Overview

The goal of this site is to provide the CMS Security and Privacy community with the information and best practices needed to launch software from a technical and compliance perspective.

We know that compliance and security are not often the first things that come to mind when creating new, innovative solutions. But embracing and internalizing these processes early and throughout your development cycle will ensure your project is cheaper, less confusing, and more secure in the long run.

How did we get here?

The Federal Information Security Management (FISMA) Act was passed in 2002. FISMA requires all federal agencies to have information security plans in place to protect sensitive information. Since it’s passage, all agencies must have a set of processes and system controls designed to ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of system-related information.

What is a system?

A federal information system—"system"—is an integrated set of components for collecting, storing, and processing data. Systems provide information and knowledge in the form of digital products. Examples include giant platforms like Sharepoint and Salesforce, and smaller tools like Airtable and Mural.

How does a system show it is secure?

Every system must go through the National Institute of Standard and Technology (NIST) Risk Management Framework before it can be used to process federal information. This process culminates in a signed Authority to Operate (ATO) being issued.

When to start working on an ATO?

Because the ATO process is a complex, multi-step process that will constrain the design and implementation of your system, you should start thinking about how it applies to your system before you begin designing and implementing it. Starting the ATO process after you’ve already invested in development can result in costly delays and painful rework.

How to use this site

This site should be used early and often throughout your system development lifecycle, especially when you’re starting to consider a future project launch or feature release. This not only ensures the long-term protection of sensitive information, it also prevents costly, duplicative effort after the project’s completion.

To better understand and prepare for key points in the ATO process and align your system development lifecycle appropriately, it’s helpful to think about the ATO in phases:

  • Initiate
  • Develop and Assess
  • Operate
  • Retire

While every project and process is unique, every successful ATO goes through these four basic phases to ensure that a system is secure and compliant.

Phases

Initiate

The Initiate phase documents the general business need that the system intends to address, and provides business owners with acceptable solutions.

This process relies heavily on the Business Owner, the Enterprise Architecture (EA) team, Navigator, Subject Matter Experts (SMEs), and the Governance Review Team (GRT) to develop, document, and evaluate potential options for development.

Representatives from Security, Privacy, and Accessibility must be consulted by the project in the Initiate phase, and the Security Assessment and Authorization process begins.

Develop and Access

The Develop and Access phase creates user stories or requirements, designs and develops the solution, deploys to a non-production environment, and tests for compliance with the requirements and CMS standards so that it is production-ready.

Operate

The Operate phase is to maintain steady production operations while ensuring that routine maintenance is performed and sound security practices are maintained.

Upgrades, system software patches, hardware upgrades, and modifications to interfaces with other systems are all maintenance issues that must be supported throughout this phase.

Retire

The Retire phase ensures compliance with Federal guidelines when retiring a government IT system. There are many aspects to consider such as records retention, information security, and investment close-out procedures.
